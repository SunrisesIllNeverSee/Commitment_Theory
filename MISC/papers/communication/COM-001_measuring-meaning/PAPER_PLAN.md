## Abstract

Can meaning be measured? Communication theory has long maintained that meaning is contextual, interpretive, and irreducibly social — a product of the relationship between sender, receiver, channel, and context that resists formal quantification. CT offers a partial but consequential answer: while the full semantic surplus of a message is not quantifiable, its commitment kernel — the deontic content that constitutes the action-binding portion of the message — is operationally measurable and conserved under governed transformation. This paper introduces CT's measurement framework to communication scholars and argues that the commitment kernel is the communication-theoretically load-bearing portion of a message: the part whose preservation or loss determines whether the message achieves its communicative function in legal, regulatory, contractual, and high-stakes administrative contexts. The paper makes three contributions to communication theory. First, it provides the first operational measurement framework for semantic fidelity in AI-mediated communication — a framework grounded in a falsifiable physical law and an empirical record. Second, it argues that AI-mediated communication introduces a new category of communicative failure — commitment drift — that is structurally distinct from noise, distortion, and misinterpretation and requires a new theoretical framework to characterize. Third, it connects CT's entropy framework to Shannon's original information-theoretic account of communication, completing the bridge that Shannon deliberately left open by defining information as what survives the channel and leaving meaning aside.

## Outline

I. **Introduction: The Measurement Problem in Communication Theory** — Frames the measurement question; acknowledges communication theory's resistance to formal quantification; argues that CT offers a principled partial answer that is sufficient for the most consequential communicative functions.

II. **Commitment Kernel as the Load-Bearing Component of Communication** — Argues that in legal, regulatory, contractual, and administrative communication, the commitment kernel is the communicatively functional core; its preservation or loss determines whether the message achieves its purpose.

III. **AI-Mediated Communication and Commitment Drift** — Introduces commitment drift as a new category of communicative failure in AI-mediated communication; distinguishes it from noise, distortion, and misinterpretation; explains why existing communication theory frameworks do not capture it.

IV. **The Measurement Framework** — Introduces the CT measurement harness for a communication theory audience; describes the oracle (NLI bidirectional entailment), the conservation protocol, and the failure taxonomy in communication-theoretic terms.

V. **Shannon Completed: Meaning as What Survives the Governed Channel** — Develops the Shannon bridge: Shannon defined information as what survives the channel; CT defines commitment as what survives the governed transformation channel; the two frameworks together provide a complete account of communicative fidelity from bits to meaning.

VI. **Empirical Results in Communication Terms** — Reports the CT experimental record (EXP-003, EXP-007) in terms communication scholars can use; discusses what the results reveal about AI-mediated communication fidelity.

VII. **Implications for Communication Theory and AI-Mediated Communication Research** — Identifies the most important implications for communication theory: the need for commitment-aware frameworks for AI-mediated communication; the empirical measurement protocol as a research tool for communication scholars.

## Key Claims

- The commitment kernel is the communication-theoretically load-bearing portion of a message in legal, regulatory, contractual, and administrative contexts — its preservation determines whether the message achieves its communicative function.
- Commitment drift is a new category of communicative failure in AI-mediated communication, structurally distinct from noise, distortion, and misinterpretation, and requiring a new theoretical framework to characterize.
- CT provides the first operational measurement framework for semantic fidelity in AI-mediated communication, grounded in a falsifiable physical law and an empirical record.
- Shannon's framework and CT's framework are complementary: Shannon defines information as what survives the channel (bits); CT defines commitment as what survives the governed transformation channel (meaning); together they provide a complete account of communicative fidelity.
- The CT measurement protocol is a research tool that communication scholars can use to study semantic fidelity in AI-mediated communication — a new empirical methodology for a longstanding theoretical question.
