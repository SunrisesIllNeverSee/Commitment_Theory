## Abstract

The Second Law of Semantic Entropy states that under ungoverned transformation, the commitment kernel of a semantic object decays monotonically (ΔH_C > 0). This is a physical law, not a social one — it describes the behavior of semantic objects regardless of who is affected. But its social distribution is not neutral. When AI systems transform legal provisions, medical instructions, bureaucratic communications, and contractual terms, commitment decay does not fall equally across the populations who interact with these systems. Populations who interact with AI transformation systems through interfaces with lower governance density — unrepresented parties, non-English speakers, pro se litigants, benefits claimants, workers without legal counsel — bear disproportionate exposure to commitment drift. This paper argues that the Second Law, analyzed through the lens of science and technology studies, reveals AI's meaning-degradation capacity as a mechanism of epistemic inequality: a structural process by which the action-binding content of legal and social commitments is more likely to be lost in the processing pipelines that serve the less powerful than in those that serve the more powerful. The paper develops three STS-oriented arguments. First, commitment drift is not random — it is systematically distributed by governance density, which is itself distributed by resources, representation, and institutional power. Second, the nine failure modes are not equally dangerous for all populations — negation reversal and exception dropping are specifically devastating for claimants whose entire legal position depends on a single exception clause or a specific prohibition. Third, a governance solution exists — CT's CCR — and the fact that it has not been implemented is itself a sociotechnical fact that STS is positioned to analyze.

## Outline

I. **Introduction: A Physical Law with a Social Distribution** — Introduces the Second Law of Semantic Entropy as a physical law and immediately asks: who bears its costs?

II. **Governance Density as a Social Variable** — Demonstrates that governance density — the ratio of constraint operations to transformation operations — is not uniformly distributed; it correlates with resources, institutional power, and representation.

III. **Who Uses Ungoverned AI Systems?** — Profiles the populations most exposed to ungoverned or low-governance AI transformation systems: unrepresented parties in administrative proceedings, pro se litigants, benefits claimants, non-English speakers, workers without legal counsel.

IV. **The Failure Modes Are Not Equally Dangerous** — Analyzes which failure modes are most consequential for each population type; argues that exception dropping and negation reversal are specifically devastating for claimants whose legal position depends on a single clause or prohibition.

V. **Commitment Drift as a Mechanism of Epistemic Inequality** — Develops the theoretical argument: commitment drift is a structural mechanism by which meaning is more likely to be lost in the pipelines that serve the less powerful — an epistemic injustice at the system level.

VI. **The CCR as a Sociotechnical Object** — Analyzes the Commitment Conservation Requirement as a sociotechnical solution and asks why it has not been implemented; frames the governance gap as a political and institutional question, not merely a technical one.

VII. **Conclusion: STS and the Conservation Law** — Argues that STS is positioned to contribute to CT's research program by analyzing the social distribution of commitment drift and the political economy of governance density.

## Key Claims

- The Second Law of Semantic Entropy is a physical law with a social distribution: commitment decay falls disproportionately on populations who interact with AI systems through low-governance interfaces.
- Governance density is a social variable: it correlates with resources, institutional power, and representation; low-governance AI systems are disproportionately deployed in the contexts that serve the less powerful.
- Commitment drift is a mechanism of epistemic inequality: it systematically degrades the action-binding content of legal and social commitments in the pipelines that serve the less powerful more than in those that serve the more powerful.
- Exception dropping and negation reversal are the failure modes most devastating for populations whose legal position depends on a single exception clause or prohibition — and these are also the failure modes most invisible to standard review.
- The governance solution (CCR) exists but has not been implemented; the gap between the law's requirement and its implementation is a sociotechnical fact that requires STS analysis, not just technical remediation.

## Writing Notes

**Add an empirical anchor — at least one concrete case (Section III or IV):**
STS reviewers will reject a purely theoretical paper without empirical grounding. Identify one real-world case where AI-mediated transformation of legal/bureaucratic text has produced measurable harm to a specific population:
- Candidate: automated benefits determination systems (e.g., Michigan's MiDAS unemployment fraud detection system, which falsely accused tens of thousands of claimants) — analyze through the CT framework as commitment drift in the eligibility determination process
- Alternative: AI-assisted immigration document processing — where translation/summarization of asylum claims could produce commitment drift affecting the outcome of adjudication
The case study does not need to run the full CT harness — it needs to demonstrate that the theoretical framework (governance density → commitment drift → population impact) maps onto a real-world instance.

**Engage FATE/FAccT literature explicitly (Section V or VI):**
STS audiences read the FATE (Fairness, Accountability, Transparency, Ethics) literature. Engage:
- Barocas & Selbst (2016) on disparate impact in ML — CT adds a new mechanism (commitment drift) that is distinct from algorithmic bias but produces similar distributional effects
- Benjamin (2019) *Race After Technology* — CT's governance density analysis supports Benjamin's argument that ostensibly neutral technical systems encode structural inequality
- Noble (2018) *Algorithms of Oppression* — commitment drift in search/summarization systems is an instance of Noble's critique applied to legal meaning specifically

**Clarify Claim 5 — normative or descriptive:**
Key Claim 4 states "exception dropping and negation reversal are the failure modes most devastating for populations whose legal position depends on a single exception clause or prohibition." This is a descriptive claim about the differential impact of specific failure modes. But the paper's broader argument — that commitment drift is a mechanism of epistemic inequality — is normative. Section VI ("The CCR as a Sociotechnical Object") must explicitly state whether it is making a policy recommendation (normative: implement the CCR to reduce inequality) or a descriptive observation (the CCR exists but has not been implemented, and STS should study why). Recommended: both, in sequence. First the descriptive observation, then the normative implication.
