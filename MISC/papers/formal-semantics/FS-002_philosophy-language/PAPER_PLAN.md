## Abstract

Philosophy of language has long asked what meaning is — what makes an utterance meaningful, what determines its truth conditions, what role context plays in interpretation. CT raises a prior question: what survives? When a semantic object is transformed — translated, summarized, paraphrased, reformatted, recursively processed by an AI system — some of its content is conserved and some is lost. The Conservation Law of Commitment identifies the invariant: the commitment kernel, the deontic core that constitutes the text's action-binding content. This paper argues that what survives transformation is not an arbitrary remnant but a philosophically significant entity — and that CT's discovery of a conservation law for it is not merely an empirical finding in computational linguistics but a contribution to the philosophy of language. The paper develops three philosophical theses. First, meaning has an invariant core that is revealed by the act of transformation rather than defined prior to it — a deflationary thesis about meaning that sidesteps rather than resolves the debate between semantic externalism and internalism. Second, the conservation of commitment under governed transformation is not a contingent fact about particular AI systems but a structural property of semantic objects that constrains what any transformation system can do — an essentialist thesis about semantic content. Third, the Second Law of Semantic Entropy — that commitment decays under ungoverned transformation — is not a claim about cognitive processing or communicative intention but a claim about the behavior of semantic objects in transformation environments — a claim that philosophy of language must account for.

## Outline

I. **Introduction: What Philosophy of Language Has Not Asked** — Frames the transformation question as a gap in philosophy of language; argues that the question "what survives?" is prior to the question "what does it mean?"

II. **The Invariant Core: Deflating the Meaning Debate** — Develops the deflationary thesis: CT sidesteps the externalism/internalism debate by defining commitment as what survives transformation rather than as what is determined by the mind or the world.

III. **Conservation as Structural Property** — Develops the essentialist thesis: commitment conservation is a structural property of semantic objects; connects to debates about semantic holism vs. atomism and the analytic/synthetic distinction.

IV. **The Second Law and Philosophy of Language** — Develops the entropy thesis: the Second Law makes a claim about the behavior of semantic objects in transformation environments that philosophy of language must accommodate — meaning is not stable by default.

V. **CT and the Gricean Program** — Examines the relationship between CT's commitment kernel and Grice's natural vs. non-natural meaning; between CT's conservation law and Grice's cooperative principle; argues that CT's framework complements rather than competes with the Gricean approach.

VI. **Implications for Semantic Normativity** — Discusses CT's implications for the normativity of meaning: if commitment conservation is a structural law, then systems that violate it are not just pragmatically deficient but semantically defective.

VII. **Conclusion: A New Question for Philosophy of Language** — Argues that CT's conservation law adds a new question to philosophy of language's agenda — "what survives?" — alongside the existing questions of "what does it mean?" and "how is it interpreted?"

## Key Claims

- What survives transformation is a philosophically significant entity — the commitment kernel, the deontic core of a semantic object — not an arbitrary remnant; CT's discovery of a conservation law for it is a contribution to philosophy of language.
- CT sidesteps the externalism/internalism debate by defining commitment as what survives transformation rather than as what is determined by the mind or world — a deflationary move analogous to Shannon's sidestepping of "what is information?"
- Commitment conservation is a structural property of semantic objects that constrains what any transformation system can do — an essentialist thesis that is empirically grounded and falsifiable.
- The Second Law of Semantic Entropy is not a claim about cognitive processing or communicative intention but about the behavior of semantic objects in transformation environments; philosophy of language must accommodate it.
- CT adds a new question to philosophy of language's agenda — "what survives?" — that is prior to both the interpretation question and the reference question.

## Writing Notes

**FS-002 depends on FS-001 — do not begin writing until FS-001's canonical invariant is formalized:**
FS-002's three philosophical theses (deflationary, essentialist, entropy) all presuppose the formal definition of the canonical invariant that FS-001 provides. Without FS-001's Section IV (formal definition), FS-002's philosophical arguments rest on an undefined term.

**Engage semantic minimalism and contextualism explicitly (Section II):**
The deflationary thesis (CT sidesteps externalism/internalism) will be tested by both camps:
- Borg's minimal semantics: CT's canonical invariant is closer to Borg's minimal content than to context-enriched content. If CT's commitment kernel is grammar-and-lexicon-determinable, that supports semantic minimalism. Engage this alliance explicitly.
- Recanati's contextualism: Recanati would argue that the "deontic content" of a legal provision depends on pragmatic context (legislative intent, regulatory context, judicial interpretation). CT's response: the commitment kernel is not the full meaning — it is the transformation-invariant core. Pragmatic enrichment operates on top of the kernel, not underneath it.
- Cappelen & Lepore's insensitivity thesis: if CT's kernel is genuinely context-independent, it supports their argument against pervasive context-sensitivity. This is a stronger alliance than Borg and should be noted.

**State what CT does to Gricean cooperativity (Section V):**
The paper must explicitly address: does CT's conservation law presuppose or undermine Grice's Cooperative Principle? Answer: CT is orthogonal to cooperativity. The conservation law applies regardless of whether the transformation agent is cooperative, adversarial, or indifferent. This distinguishes CT from Gricean pragmatics, which assumes cooperative speakers. The Second Law (commitment decay under ungoverned transformation) holds even for cooperative AI systems — cooperation does not guarantee conservation.
