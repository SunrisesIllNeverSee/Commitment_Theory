## Abstract

Commitment Theory is a formal framework for the governance of semantic content under transformation. This monograph presents the complete theory — its axioms, laws, measurement science, and applications across law, information science, AI governance, communication theory, economics, and philosophy of language — in a unified treatment addressed to an interdisciplinary scholarly audience. The central contribution of CT is a conservation law: under governed transformation, the commitment kernel of any semantic object — its deontic core, the obligations, prohibitions, permissions, and modal constraints that constitute its action-binding content — is conserved. Under ungoverned transformation, it decays monotonically. This is not a design aspiration or a normative preference; it is a discovered physical law with a falsifiable statement, an empirical record, and a public measurement protocol. The monograph is organized in four parts. Part I establishes the theoretical foundations: the McHenry Axioms (Layer -1), the Six-Gate Protocol (Layer 0), the Conservation Law and Second Law (Layer 1), and the measurement science (Papers 1–5). Part II presents the enforcement architecture: MO§ES™, SIGSYSTEM, and the governance density framework. Part III presents the applications across all tracks: legal (L-001 through L-010), computational linguistics (CL-001, CL-002), formal semantics (FS-001, FS-002), information science (IS-001, IS-002), sociology/STS (STS-001), communication theory (COM-001), economics (ECON-001), and AI governance (GOV-001). Part IV presents the extensions: the Post-Turing Test, the Semantic Channel Capacity Theorem (CAP-001), and the open questions that define CT's next generation of research. The monograph concludes with the invitation that has always anchored CT's program: falsify it.

## Key Claims

- The monograph presents the first unified treatment of CT across all tracks and layers, demonstrating that the Conservation Law is the single foundation from which the legal, computational, philosophical, and governance applications all derive.
- CT's claim is precisely delimited: it governs the transformation of semantic commitment, not the interpretation of meaning, the resolution of legal disputes, or the design choices of AI systems — those remain human questions.
- The monograph is addressed to an interdisciplinary audience because CT's implications are interdisciplinary — no single disciplinary framework can contain them, and any framework that claims to will miss the point.
- CT's deepest theoretical contribution is methodological: it sidesteps "what is meaning?" the same way Shannon sidestepped "what is information?" — by defining the invariant as what survives, and measuring it.
- The monograph concludes with an invitation to falsify, not a declaration of completion: CT is a research program in progress, and the most important work remains to be done.
