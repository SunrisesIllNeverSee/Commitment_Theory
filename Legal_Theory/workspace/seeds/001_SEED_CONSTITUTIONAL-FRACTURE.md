## The Fracture

A lawyer uses AI to research case law, analyze evidence, map charges to statutory elements, anticipate opposing strategy, and prepare documentation for a legal proceeding. That work — every prompt, every output, every strategic memo generated through the AI system — is protected as attorney work product. It cannot be discovered, subpoenaed, or compelled. The protection is absolute within the scope of the work product doctrine.

A non-lawyer uses the same AI system, on the same platform, to perform the same research, the same analysis, the same mapping, the same strategic preparation, producing the same quality of output. Under the current case law — specifically United States v. Heppner (S.D.N.Y., Feb. 17, 2026) — that work is not protected. It is discoverable. It can be seized, subpoenaed, and used against the person who created it.

Same platform. Same prompts. Same work. Same output. Different protection. The variable is not the quality of the work, the nature of the analysis, the reliability of the output, or the purpose for which it was created. The variable is the credential of the person who typed the prompt.

The protection follows the license, not the work.

## The Rulings

Three federal rulings issued within eight weeks of each other in early 2026 define the current landscape.

### United States v. Heppner — S.D.N.Y., February 10/17, 2026

Criminal securities fraud case. Defendant Bradley Heppner, a CEO, used consumer Claude (Anthropic) — without his attorney's direction — to generate 31 documents analyzing his legal situation: defense strategies, factual analyses, legal arguments. FBI seized the documents during a search warrant. Defense counsel asserted attorney-client privilege and work product protection. Judge Jed S. Rakoff denied both.

Reasoning: Claude is not an attorney and cannot form an attorney-client relationship. The consumer platform's privacy policy disclaimed confidentiality and permitted data sharing with third parties including government authorities. Heppner acted on his own initiative without counsel's direction. The documents did not reflect counsel's mental impressions or litigation strategy. Sending the documents to his attorney after creating them did not retroactively create privilege.

The court characterized the question as "a question of first impression nationwide." The written opinion stated: "the core purpose of the Work Product Doctrine is to protect the mental strategies of counsel in anticipation of litigation."

The court left open that AI use directed by counsel — under a Kovel-type arrangement — might qualify for protection. But the holding as written draws the line at attorney involvement: present = protected, absent = exposed.

### Warner v. Gilbarco, Inc. — E.D. Mich., February 10, 2026

Civil employment discrimination case. Pro se plaintiff Sohyon Warner used consumer ChatGPT to assist with litigation preparation. Defendants moved to compel production of all AI-related materials. Magistrate Judge Anthony P. Patti denied the motion.

Reasoning: the materials were protected work product under FRCP 26(b)(3)(A) because the rule protects "documents and tangible things that are prepared in anticipation of litigation or for trial by another party or its representative." The pro se plaintiff was "simultaneously the party and the advocate" — her AI-assisted materials reflected her own mental impressions as the person litigating the case. Using a public AI tool did not waive protection because "generative AI programs are tools, not persons" and disclosure to them is not disclosure to an adversary. The court called the defendants' request "a fishing expedition" and stated that compelling production "would nullify work-product protection in nearly every modern drafting environment, a result no court has endorsed."

### Morgan v. V2X, Inc. — D. Colo., March 30, 2026

Civil case. Another pro se plaintiff. Same result as Warner. Work product protection upheld for AI-assisted materials. The court distinguished Heppner on the grounds that it was a criminal case not governed by the Federal Rules of Civil Procedure and involved a represented party acting apart from counsel — whereas a pro se litigant has no such "gap" between party and advocate.

## The Asymmetry the Rulings Create

The three rulings together produce the following protection map:

**A lawyer uses AI → Protected.** The prompts reflect counsel's mental impressions. Tremblay v. OpenAI (N.D. Cal. 2024) and Concord Music Group v. Anthropic (N.D. Cal. 2025) held that AI prompts crafted by attorneys constitute opinion work product. Established and unchallenged.

**A pro se civil litigant uses AI → Protected (in the Sixth Circuit and D. Colorado).** Warner and Morgan held that when the person IS their own lawyer, their AI use reflects their own mental impressions as the advocate. Not yet tested in other circuits.

**A represented criminal defendant uses AI independently → Not protected (in the Second Circuit).** Heppner held that without attorney direction, the defendant's AI work does not reflect counsel's mental impressions and is unprotected. The court explicitly rejected the holding of Shih v. Petal Card, Inc. (S.D.N.Y. 2021), which had extended work product protection to materials prepared by a party in anticipation of litigation regardless of attorney involvement.

**A crime victim uses AI → No ruling. No framework. No protection. No discussion.**

**An advocate for a crime victim uses AI → No ruling. No framework. No protection. No discussion.**

**An indigent defendant who cannot afford an attorney and uses AI before counsel is appointed → No ruling. The question has been identified in one paragraph (Clio, February 2026) but not adjudicated.**

**A person preparing to exercise any constitutional right (petition, defense, due process, court access) uses AI → No ruling. No framework. The constitutional dimension has not been raised.**

## The Constitutional Argument

Every constitutional right that requires preparation to exercise is structurally conditioned on the ability to afford a lawyer — because only a lawyer's preparation is protected.

The First Amendment guarantees the right to petition the government for redress of grievances. Exercising that right effectively requires research, documentation, analysis, and strategic preparation. When a non-lawyer performs that preparation using AI and the system strips protection from the work, the right to petition is subordinated to the economic ability to hire an attorney.

The Sixth Amendment guarantees the right to counsel — and implicitly, through Faretta v. California (1975), the right to represent oneself. But Heppner holds that a represented defendant's independent AI preparation is unprotected. And no ruling addresses an indigent defendant's AI preparation before counsel is appointed. The right to prepare one's own defense — which Faretta protects as a constitutional right — is functionally negated if the preparation is discoverable.

The Fourteenth Amendment guarantees equal protection. The current framework creates two classes of person performing identical work with asymmetric protection. The classification (attorney credential vs. no credential) does not serve the government interest the doctrine claims to protect. The stated interest behind work product protection is encouraging thorough case preparation to preserve the adversarial system's integrity. That interest applies to every person in the legal system. Limiting the protection to credentialed attorneys serves only the guild's interest in maintaining the credential as a prerequisite for full participation in the legal system. AI has made the credential unnecessary for producing attorney-quality work. The doctrine has not adapted.

The Fifth and Fourteenth Amendments guarantee due process. A fair proceeding requires a structurally level playing field. When one side's preparation is shielded and the other side's is exposed — based not on the content or quality of the work but on the credentials of the person who performed it — the playing field is constitutionally tilted.

## The Sprawl

The fracture radiates outward from Heppner into at least eight adjacent domains:

**1. Criminal defense.** Indigent defendants who use AI before counsel is appointed. Defendants who supplement appointed counsel's work with their own AI research. Jailhouse lawyers using AI. All currently unprotected or ambiguously protected.

**2. Crime victims.** Not parties, not represented by the DA (who represents the state), not entitled to appointed counsel, not pro se. Exist in a structural gap where no existing work product framework attaches.

**3. Civil pro se litigants.** Warner and Morgan provide protection in the Sixth Circuit and D. Colorado. Not tested in other circuits. Heppner's Second Circuit reasoning would deny protection if applied to a represented party's independent AI use. The split will reach the appellate courts.

**4. Administrative proceedings.** Persons navigating immigration, benefits, disability, housing, licensing, and regulatory proceedings without counsel. AI preparation for administrative hearings faces the same protection asymmetry.

**5. Victims' rights frameworks.** Marsy's Law (12 states, Tennessee ballot measure November 2026), the federal Crime Victims' Rights Act (18 U.S.C. § 3771), state victims' bills of rights. None include work product protection for victim-prepared materials. None address AI-specific protections.

**6. Access to justice movement.** The broader effort to reduce barriers to legal services for underserved populations. AI is the most powerful access-to-justice tool ever created. The current protection framework penalizes its use by the population it could most benefit.

**7. AI governance.** The question of how AI-assisted work should be treated in legal proceedings is a governance question. It requires a protocol — a set of rules determining when AI output is protected, when it is discoverable, and what factors govern the distinction. The current answer (credential of the user) is arbitrary and constitutionally suspect. A governance framework based on the nature and purpose of the work — regardless of who performed it — would be constitutionally sound and technologically appropriate.
