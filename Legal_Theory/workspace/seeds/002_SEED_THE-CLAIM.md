# DOC 002 | THE CLAIM

**Date:** 2026-04-20  
**Document ID:** 002_SEED_THE-CLAIM  

---

The United States Constitution guarantees rights that require preparation to exercise — the right to petition the government, the right to defend oneself, the right to due process, the right to access the courts, the right to self-representation. The legal system protects that preparation through the work product doctrine, which shields litigation-related analysis, strategy, and mental impressions from discovery by the opposing party.

That protection is conditioned on a credential. When a licensed attorney performs the preparation — or directs it — the work is protected absolutely. When a non-lawyer performs identical preparation, producing identical quality analysis on the same platform using the same tools, the work is not protected. It is discoverable, seizable, and usable against the person who created it.

AI has broken the capability barrier between lawyers and non-lawyers. A non-lawyer using a consumer AI system can now perform legal research, statutory analysis, evidence mapping, defense strategy anticipation, and case preparation at a level that matches or exceeds what many attorneys produce. The quality of the work is equivalent. The nature of the work is equivalent. The purpose of the work — preparing to exercise a constitutional right — is equivalent.

The protection is not equivalent.

On February 10, 2026, Judge Jed S. Rakoff of the Southern District of New York ruled in United States v. Heppner that a non-lawyer's AI-generated legal analysis is not protected by attorney-client privilege or the work product doctrine. On the same day, Magistrate Judge Anthony P. Patti of the Eastern District of Michigan ruled in Warner v. Gilbarco that a pro se litigant's AI-generated legal analysis IS protected as work product. The split has widened with Morgan v. V2X (D. Colo., March 30, 2026), which sided with Warner. The question will reach the circuit courts.

None of these rulings address the full scope of the problem. The protection asymmetry affects every person who exercises a constitutional right requiring preparation and uses AI as the preparation tool without attorney direction: crime victims preparing to report, indigent defendants before counsel is appointed, persons navigating administrative proceedings, advocates, self-represented civil litigants, and any person exercising any constitutional right that requires structured preparation.

The asymmetry is compounded by the structural inequality of the tools themselves. The legal profession has built proprietary AI platforms — Harvey (built on Claude/Anthropic), CoCounsel (built on GPT-4/OpenAI), and others — available exclusively or primarily to licensed attorneys by paid subscription. These platforms use the same underlying AI models as the consumer platforms available to the public. The same model weights process an attorney's privileged strategy memo on Harvey and a non-lawyer's identical analysis on consumer Claude. The enterprise agreement between the wrapper company and the model provider adds a contractual confidentiality layer — but the underlying architecture, the model, the infrastructure, and the company processing the data are the same. The protection distinction is contractual, not computational. It is economic, not constitutional.

The claim is this: conditioning the protection of constitutional preparation on a professional credential — when AI has made the credential functionally unnecessary for producing the protected work — violates the Equal Protection Clause of the Fourteenth Amendment, the Due Process Clauses of the Fifth and Fourteenth Amendments, the right to petition under the First Amendment, and the right to self-representation under the Sixth Amendment as interpreted in Faretta v. California. The protection must follow the work, not the license. When it follows the license, the Constitution's guarantees are available only to those who can afford the credentialed wrapper — and that is a pay-gated constitutional protection scheme applied to fundamental rights.
