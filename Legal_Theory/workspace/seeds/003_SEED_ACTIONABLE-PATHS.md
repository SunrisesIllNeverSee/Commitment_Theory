### 5. NYS Legislative Proposal — Within 60 Days

Targeted amendment to CPL § 245.65. Current text protects "legal research, opinions, theories or conclusions of the adverse party or its attorney or the attorney's agents." Proposed amendment adds: "or of any person preparing materials in anticipation of reporting a crime, participating in a criminal proceeding, or exercising rights under this chapter, regardless of whether such person is a licensed attorney."

This is a narrow, single-section amendment. It is consistent with the NYS legislature's demonstrated trajectory on victims' rights (Fair Access to Victim Compensation Act, Melanie's Law, Hope Card Act — all enacted 2024-2025). It does not require a constitutional amendment. It does not create new rights. It extends an existing protection to a population the original statute excluded because the scenario (non-lawyer performing attorney-quality legal preparation) did not exist when the statute was written. AI created the scenario. The statute needs to catch up.

Draft the bill. Identify the sponsor. The NYS legislative session runs January through June. The 2027 session is the target.

### 6. Federal Legislative Proposal — Within 6 Months

Amendment to the Crime Victims' Rights Act (18 U.S.C. § 3771) adding a subsection: a crime victim's case preparation materials — including materials prepared with AI assistance — prepared in anticipation of reporting a crime or participating in a criminal proceeding, shall be treated as work product and shall not be discoverable by the defendant, subject to the same exceptions and judicial oversight applicable to attorney work product under Federal Rule of Civil Procedure 26(b)(3).

Broader amendment to the Federal Rules of Criminal Procedure (paralleling FRCP 26(b)(3) for civil cases) extending work product protection to any person's litigation-related preparation materials regardless of professional credential, when prepared in anticipation of litigation or participation in a legal proceeding.

### 7. Challenge the Contracts — Within 6 Months

File FOIA requests, public records requests, or journalistic inquiries to determine: what are the actual contractual terms between Harvey and Anthropic, between CoCounsel and OpenAI, and between other legal AI wrappers and their underlying model providers? Specifically: do the contracts address whether model weights trained on or influenced by enterprise customer data are shared across the consumer platform? Do they guarantee architectural segregation or only policy-level data handling commitments? Is the "confidentiality" that attorneys rely on for work product protection actually a computational guarantee or merely a contractual promise that can be breached by the model provider, subpoenaed by a government authority, or rendered meaningless by the model's architecture?

If the contracts do not address these questions — and the reasonable expectation is that they do not — then the entire legal AI industry's work product protection is built on an untested contractual assumption, not on an architectural guarantee. Publishing this finding would put every law firm using AI on notice that their work product protection may be less solid than they believe — and would create immediate pressure for both the AI companies and the legal profession to address the gap.

### 8. Build the Constitutional AI Access Layer — Ongoing

The argument identifies a missing piece in the legal system: a free, confidentiality-guaranteed AI legal preparation tool available to every person exercising a constitutional right. This does not exist. Building it — or advocating for its creation — is the constructive counterpart to the critical argument. MO§E§™'s governance protocol is architecturally relevant here: a constitutional governance layer that guarantees signal integrity, provenance, and confidentiality for AI-assisted legal preparation, available to any user regardless of credential or economic status, with work product protection built into the architecture rather than dependent on a contractual wrapper.

---

### 9. Appellate Test Case — When It Arises

When a court proceeding produces a motion to compel a non-lawyer's AI-generated legal preparation materials, challenge the motion on constitutional grounds. Argue that stripping work product protection based on the preparer's credential — when the work is identical in quality, nature, and purpose to attorney-protected work — violates equal protection, due process, and the right to self-representation. The motion, the opposition, the ruling, and the appeal create the case law that does not currently exist. The public brief (Path 1), the academic paper (Path 2), and the advocacy organization contacts (Path 3) build the intellectual infrastructure that supports the constitutional challenge when it arrives.
