# DOC 004 | THE LANDSCAPE BEFORE AI

## Part 1 — The Work Product Doctrine: Origin, Scope, and Who It Covers

### Origin

Hickman v. Taylor, 329 U.S. 495 (1947). The Supreme Court created the work product doctrine to protect "the mental processes of the attorney" from discovery. The case involved written statements an attorney obtained from witnesses during litigation preparation. The Court held that requiring disclosure "would create serious potential for demoralizing the legal profession" and would mean "much of what is now put down in writing would remain unwritten." The doctrine exists to encourage thorough preparation by guaranteeing that the adversary cannot free-ride on the other side's analytical work.

Federal Rule of Civil Procedure 26(b)(3) codified the doctrine. It protects "documents and tangible things that are prepared in anticipation of litigation or for trial by or for another party or its representative (including the other party's attorney, consultant, surety, indemnitor, insurer, or agent)."

New York's criminal equivalent is CPL § 245.65: "This article does not authorize discovery by a party of those portions of records, reports, correspondence, memoranda, or internal documents of the adverse party which are only the legal research, opinions, theories or conclusions of the adverse party or its attorney or the attorney's agents."

### Who the doctrine protects — pre-AI

**Attorneys.** Absolute protection for mental impressions, legal theories, case strategy, research notes, analysis memos, interview preparation, witness assessment, evidence evaluation. No exception. The attorney's thought process is inviolable.

**Attorney's agents.** Paralegals, law clerks, investigators, consultants, expert witnesses retained by counsel — all covered as extensions of the attorney's work. Their work product is the attorney's work product by agency. The protection follows the chain of direction from the attorney outward.

**Parties acting as their own advocates (pro se).** FRCP 26(b)(3) protects materials prepared "by or for another party or its representative." Multiple federal courts have held that a pro se litigant qualifies as "another party" whose litigation preparation is protected. Anderson v. Furst, No. 2:17-12676 (E.D. Mich. 2019). Shih v. Petal Card, Inc., 565 F. Supp. 3d 557 (S.D.N.Y. 2021) — though Heppner expressly rejected Shih. The protection for pro se parties is contested and circuit-dependent, but it existed pre-AI.

### Who the doctrine does NOT protect — pre-AI

**Non-lawyer third parties acting independently.** A friend who researches case law for you. A family member who takes notes on your behalf. An advocate who organizes your evidence. A journalist who investigates your claims. Unless these persons are operating under attorney direction as agents, their work product is not protected. They are outside the circle of privilege.

**Crime victims.** Not parties in criminal proceedings. Not represented by the DA (who represents the state). Not entitled to appointed counsel. Not covered by any work product framework. A victim who researches the law, organizes evidence, maps charges to statutory elements, and prepares documentation for a DA meeting has no protection for any of that work. The defense can, in principle, seek it through discovery if it becomes known to exist.

**Indigent defendants before counsel is appointed.** The Sixth Amendment right to counsel attaches at the initiation of adversary judicial proceedings — formal charge, preliminary hearing, indictment, information, or arraignment (Kirby v. Illinois, 406 U.S. 682 (1972)). Before that moment, a suspect or target under investigation who cannot afford counsel has no attorney, no agent relationship, and no work product protection for their own preparation. Any notes, research, or analysis they create during the pre-charging investigation period is unprotected.

### The attorney's process: what they never have to disclose

An attorney's internal process is absolutely shielded. This includes:

— Which statutes they researched and why
— Which case law they read and how they interpreted it
— Which witnesses they considered calling and why they decided against certain ones
— Their assessment of the strengths and weaknesses of their own case
— Their assessment of the opposing side's strengths and weaknesses
— Their strategic decisions about what evidence to present and in what order
— Their preparation of witnesses for testimony
— Their mock cross-examinations and trial simulations
— Their internal memos analyzing the probability of various outcomes
— Their communications with consultants, investigators, and experts
— Their notes from meetings with clients
— The analytical path they followed from raw evidence to legal theory

None of this is discoverable. None of it can be compelled. None of it can be subpoenaed. The opposing side knows the attorney prepared. The opposing side has no right to know HOW the attorney prepared, what tools they used, what analysis they performed, or what conclusions they reached internally. The protection is absolute within the scope of mental impressions and legal theories.

### Pre-AI challenges to non-attorney preparation

The question of whether a non-attorney's independent legal preparation is discoverable did arise before AI — though rarely, because non-attorneys rarely produced attorney-quality legal analysis before AI made it possible.

The relevant pre-AI scenario: a defendant or party who conducted their own legal research in a law library, took handwritten notes analyzing their case, and stored those notes privately. Were those notes discoverable?

Under the Fourth Amendment, personal papers in one's home have strong protection against unreasonable search and seizure. Boyd v. United States, 116 U.S. 616 (1886) — the Supreme Court held that compelled production of a person's private papers was equivalent to an unreasonable search. This protection has been narrowed over time but never eliminated for truly private analytical documents.

Under the Fifth Amendment, the act of producing documents can be testimonial and therefore privileged under the self-incrimination clause. Fisher v. United States, 425 U.S. 391 (1976); United States v. Hubbell, 530 U.S. 27 (2000).

Under FRCP 26(b)(3) and its state equivalents, if the person was a party to litigation and the notes were prepared in anticipation of litigation, there was at least an argument for work product protection — though it was contested for non-lawyers.

The critical pre-AI baseline: a person's private legal research notes, prepared in a library, stored in their home, had overlapping protections from the Fourth Amendment (search and seizure), the Fifth Amendment (compelled self-incrimination through production), and arguably the work product doctrine (if they were a party preparing for litigation). These protections existed regardless of the person's professional credential. The notes were protected not because the person was a lawyer but because they were private papers created through the person's own mental process.

## Part 2 — The Sixth Amendment Asymmetry

### What the defendant gets

The Sixth Amendment guarantees the accused the right to counsel in all criminal prosecutions. Gideon v. Wainwright, 372 U.S. 335 (1963) extended this right to state prosecutions. Argersinger v. Hamlin, 407 U.S. 25 (1972) extended it to any misdemeanor where imprisonment is imposed. The right attaches at the initiation of adversary judicial proceedings.

When the right attaches, the defendant receives:

— An attorney, provided by the state if the defendant cannot afford one
— Attorney-client privilege covering all communications between the defendant and counsel
— Work product protection covering all of the attorney's preparation
— The attorney's agents (investigators, paralegals, experts) are also covered
— Effective assistance of counsel — Strickland v. Washington, 466 U.S. 668 (1984) — meaning the attorney must perform competently
— The right to self-representation — Faretta v. California, 422 U.S. 806 (1975) — if the defendant chooses to waive counsel

The total protection package: free attorney + privilege + work product + effective assistance + self-representation option. This package is constitutionally guaranteed. It cannot be waived by the state or conditioned on the defendant's ability to pay.

### What the victim gets

No constitutional right to counsel. No statutory right to appointed counsel in any jurisdiction. The DA represents the state, not the victim. The victim is a witness, not a party.

Statutory rights under state victims' rights frameworks (Marsy's Law in 12 states, Executive Law Article 23 in New York, the federal Crime Victims' Rights Act) include notification, the right to be heard, the right to be present, the right to confer with the prosecution, and the right to restitution. California's Marsy's Law includes the right to be represented by counsel of the victim's own choosing — but not the right to have counsel appointed.

No state or federal framework provides the victim with:

— Appointed counsel
— Attorney-client privilege for communications with any advocate or support person (except rape crisis counselors under CPLR § 4510 and equivalent state statutes, which cover only communications with certified counselors, not with advocates, family members, or AI systems)
— Work product protection for victim-prepared case materials
— Any evidentiary shield equivalent to what the defendant's counsel receives automatically

The total protection package for the victim: zero constitutional rights to counsel, limited statutory rights to notification and participation, no privilege, no work product protection. The victim who wants privilege and work product protection must privately retain and pay for an attorney. The defendant receives these protections for free.

## Part 3 — The Tool Landscape Before AI

### Public access to legal information — pre-digital

Law libraries. Open to the public. Statute books, case reporters, legal encyclopedias, treatises, practice guides. Any person could walk into a county law library, read the same statutes and cases that attorneys read, and take notes. The information was public. The access was equal — limited only by geography (proximity to a law library), literacy, and time.

The information asymmetry between lawyers and non-lawyers was not in access to the law but in training — the ability to interpret, analyze, and apply the law to specific facts. A non-lawyer could read PL § 130.35 in a law library. Understanding how it interacts with § 130.00(7), CPL § 60.42, the Molineux doctrine, and the sentencing guidelines required legal training. The capability gap was real but the access gap was not.

### The first structural information asymmetry — Westlaw and LexisNexis

Westlaw launched in 1975. LexisNexis launched in the 1970s. Subscription-only legal research databases providing faster, deeper, more comprehensive search capability than any public law library. Attorneys gained a speed and comprehensiveness advantage over non-lawyers. The underlying information (case law, statutes, regulations) remained publicly available — Westlaw and LexisNexis didn't create proprietary law. They created proprietary access to the law's organization, searchability, and cross-referencing.

This asymmetry was tolerated without constitutional challenge because the gap was quantitative (speed, comprehensiveness) not qualitative (capability). A sufficiently dedicated non-lawyer could still find the same case law in a public library — it would just take longer. The information itself was not gated. The efficiency was gated.

### The second structural asymmetry — legal practice management tools

Clio (founded 2008), MyCase, PracticePanther, and other practice management platforms gave attorneys integrated tools for case management, document assembly, time tracking, client communication, and billing. These tools were attorney-specific — designed for law firms, sold to law firms, unavailable to non-lawyers. They didn't change the substantive legal analysis but they changed the operational efficiency of legal practice. Non-lawyers had no equivalent infrastructure for organizing legal work.

## Part 4 — What AI Changed

### The capability barrier broke

In 2022-2024, large language models (ChatGPT, Claude, Gemini) became publicly available. For the first time, a non-lawyer could type a legal question into a system and receive analysis that matched or exceeded what many attorneys produce. Not information retrieval — analysis. Statutory interpretation. Case law application. Strategic reasoning. Defense anticipation. Evidence mapping. Charge architecture.

The gap between lawyers and non-lawyers shifted from qualitative (capability) to zero. A non-lawyer using consumer AI can now perform the same intellectual work that justifies the attorney's credential and the attorney's protected status.

The protection barrier did not break. The work product doctrine still requires attorney involvement (Heppner) or party-as-own-advocate status (Warner) for protection to attach. AI broke the capability barrier but left the protection barrier intact. The result: non-lawyers can now do the work but cannot protect the work.

### The legal profession built proprietary AI wrappers

Harvey — built on Claude (Anthropic). Founded 2022. $300 million Series C from Sequoia Capital. Available to law firms by enterprise subscription. Produces attorney work product that is automatically protected because it is used within the attorney-client relationship.

CoCounsel — built on GPT-4 (OpenAI). Thomson Reuters product. Integrated with Westlaw. Available to Westlaw subscribers (attorneys and law firms). Same protection logic as Harvey.

Additional legal AI tools: Casetext (acquired by Thomson Reuters), Luminance, Relativity, Everlaw, and dozens of others. All subscription-only. All attorney-facing. All producing automatically protected output because the user is an attorney.

These platforms use the same underlying models as the consumer platforms. Harvey runs on Claude — the same Claude available at claude.ai for $20/month. CoCounsel runs on GPT-4 — the same GPT-4 available through ChatGPT. The enterprise agreements add contractual confidentiality terms. The underlying architecture, model weights, and processing infrastructure are shared.

### What did not change

The work product doctrine was not amended to account for AI. CPL § 245.65 was not amended. FRCP 26(b)(3) was not amended. No state or federal statute addresses AI-assisted legal preparation by non-lawyers. No court addressed the question until February 10, 2026 — the date Heppner and Warner were decided simultaneously with opposite outcomes.

The Sixth Amendment was not amended. No right to counsel for victims was created. No right to appointed counsel for any non-defendant was created. The constitutional asymmetry between defendants and victims remained unchanged.

The tool access asymmetry was not addressed. No free, confidentiality-guaranteed legal AI tool was created for public use. The consumer platforms (Claude, ChatGPT, Gemini) are available to all but carry privacy policies that — per Heppner — strip confidentiality from non-lawyer use. The enterprise platforms (Harvey, CoCounsel) guarantee confidentiality but are available only to credentialed subscribers. There is no constitutional AI — no tool that provides both the capability and the protection to any person exercising a constitutional right.

## Part 5 — The Baseline Summary

### Pre-AI, who had what:

**Attorneys:** Full capability (training + tools) + full protection (privilege + work product) + exclusive access to proprietary research platforms (Westlaw, LexisNexis) + practice management infrastructure.

**Attorney's agents:** Derivative protection through agency relationship. Investigators, paralegals, consultants — all covered as long as they operate under attorney direction.

**Pro se parties (civil):** Partial capability (self-taught, library access) + contested protection (FRCP 26(b)(3) covers "parties" but courts disagree on scope) + no access to proprietary tools + no practice infrastructure.

**Criminal defendants (represented):** Full capability through counsel + full protection through counsel + Sixth Amendment guarantee of counsel + Sixth Amendment guarantee of effective assistance.

**Criminal defendants (pre-appointment/indigent):** Minimal capability (library access if available, jailhouse lawyers) + no protection (no attorney, no agency, no work product) + Fourth/Fifth Amendment protections for private papers only.

**Crime victims:** No capability infrastructure + no protection + no right to counsel + no right to appointed counsel + no party status + no work product framework. The victim's only path to protection was privately retaining an attorney — an expenditure the defendant never has to make.

**General public exercising constitutional rights:** No legal capability infrastructure + no protection for legal preparation + no right to counsel outside criminal prosecution + Fourth/Fifth Amendment protections for private papers.

### Pre-AI, who had what tools:

**Attorneys:** Westlaw, LexisNexis, practice management software, legal research databases, continuing legal education, bar association resources, colleague networks.

**Everyone else:** Public law libraries. Self-help legal books. Court clerk information windows. Legal aid clinics (limited availability, means-tested, subject-matter-restricted). Pro bono attorney programs (limited availability, application-based).

### Pre-AI, what the system assumed:

The capability gap between attorneys and non-lawyers was a natural consequence of training, education, and credentialing. Non-lawyers could access the same information but could not analyze it at the same level. The work product doctrine's credential requirement (attorney or attorney's agent) was a reasonable proxy for the quality and strategic value of the preparation — because only trained professionals could produce the kind of strategic analysis worth protecting.

AI destroyed the assumption. The capability gap closed. The protection gap remained.
