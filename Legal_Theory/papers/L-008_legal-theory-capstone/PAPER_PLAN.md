## Abstract

This paper proposes a jurisprudence of the record — a systematic legal theory grounded in the Conservation Law of Commitment — as the appropriate framework for adjudicating questions of legal meaning in AI-mediated systems. Existing jurisprudential traditions — textualism, intentionalism, purposivism, legal process theory — were developed to answer the question of how courts should interpret contested statutory language in the absence of determinative legislative intent. They were not designed to answer the question of whether the statutory language that reaches a court, an agency, or a regulated party is the same statutory language that the legislature enacted. The Conservation Law of Commitment reveals that this second question — the question of semantic continuity — is now a live legal question, not a philosophical assumption. A jurisprudence of the record holds that legal meaning is not an interpretive artifact but an empirical property of the text-as-transformed: a text carries its commitment kernel or it does not, and this is measurable. The paper does not reject interpretive pluralism — CT conserves interpretive dispute rather than resolving it — but it holds that the dispute must be about the original commitment, not a commitment-drifted artifact. Drawing on the full Legal_Theory program (L-001 through L-007), the regulatory record (L-004, L-006), and the CT empirical record, the paper proposes twelve propositions for a jurisprudence of the record and defends them against the major objections from each interpretive tradition.

## Outline

I. **Introduction: What Jurisprudence Has Not Had to Ask** — Frames the question that existing jurisprudential traditions have not had to ask: is the text before the court the same text as the text from which legal meaning derives?

II. **The Conservation Law and Its Jurisprudential Stakes** — Presents the Conservation Law and Second Law as empirical facts bearing on jurisprudential assumptions; demonstrates that semantic continuity is no longer a philosophical assumption but a contingent empirical condition.

III. **A Jurisprudence of the Record: Twelve Propositions** — States the twelve propositions, parallel to P-000's structure; covers: the empirical character of legal meaning, the conservation requirement as a condition of legitimate adjudication, the failure modes as categories of legal error, the measurement standard, the preservation of interpretive pluralism, and the doctrinal implications across due process, disparate impact, and evidentiary authenticity.

IV. **Against Textualism: What the Text Actually Says** — Demonstrates that textualism's commitment to the text's plain meaning presupposes semantic continuity; shows that AI-mediated commitment drift is a textualist failure before it is an interpretive dispute.

V. **Against Intentionalism and Purposivism: Whose Intent, Which Purpose?** — Demonstrates that both traditions assume the text carries the intent or purpose that the legislature encoded; shows that commitment drift severs that assumption systematically.

VI. **The Pluralism-Preservation Claim** — Defends the claim that a jurisprudence of the record preserves and does not foreclose interpretive pluralism; the record must be accurate; what the record means remains disputed.

VII. **Conclusion: The Record as Legal Foundation** — Argues that establishing the accuracy of the legal record is the prior question that jurisprudential traditions have assumed away; CT makes it answerable.

## Key Claims

- Existing jurisprudential traditions presuppose semantic continuity — that the text before the court carries the commitment kernel that the legislature enacted — but do not provide a framework for verifying that assumption.
- The Conservation Law of Commitment reveals that semantic continuity is a contingent empirical condition, not a philosophical given; AI-mediated transformation severs it systematically.
- A jurisprudence of the record holds that legal meaning is an empirical property of the text-as-transformed: a text carries its commitment kernel or it does not, and this is measurable using the CT harness.
- A jurisprudence of the record preserves interpretive pluralism: conserving the commitment kernel does not resolve contested statutory interpretations but ensures that the contest is about the original commitment, not a drifted artifact.
- CT's twelve propositions for a jurisprudence of the record are grounded in a falsifiable physical law with an empirical record — a jurisprudential foundation unlike any that existing traditions can claim.

## Writing Notes

**Hart & Sacks — add to Section IV or V (not just citation notes):**
Hart & Sacks legal process theory argues that courts should defer to agencies and legislatures on matters within their institutional competence. CT's commitment conservation requirement raises the question: which institution should enforce it? Courts (through constitutional review), agencies (through rulemaking), or technical standards bodies (through the CCR)? The legal process answer: the institution with the competence to verify compliance — which, for CT, requires technical measurement capacity. This is the argument for a technical standard (L-003) rather than judicial enforcement alone. L-008 should engage this explicitly in Section V.

**Common law scope — add to Introduction or Section I:**
CT's commitment conservation requirement applies most directly to statutory and regulatory text — legal texts that are fixed at the moment of enactment and subject to AI-mediated application. It does not apply in the same way to common law rules, which evolve through judicial precedent. A common law rule that "changes" through a new case is not commitment drift — it is the deliberate development of law through adjudication. The scope note: "This paper addresses the conservation of commitment in fixed legal texts under AI transformation. Common law adjudication — where the 'text' evolves through case law — raises different questions that CT's framework does not yet address."

**Pluralism preservation — make this the spine of Section VI, not a subsection:**
This is the claim that will determine whether L-008 is accepted at HLR/YLJ/SLR. Those journals will read CT as technocratic imposition if they don't see that it preserves interpretive plurality. Structure Section VI as: "CT resolves the measurement question without resolving the interpretive question. Whether 'unlawful employment practice' under Title VII covers a given AI algorithm is an interpretive dispute. Whether the AI system applying that standard preserves the 'shall not discriminate' commitment kernel in the statute is a measurement question. CT addresses the measurement question and leaves the interpretive dispute intact — in fact, it makes the interpretive dispute more tractable by ensuring the thing being disputed is what was actually written."
