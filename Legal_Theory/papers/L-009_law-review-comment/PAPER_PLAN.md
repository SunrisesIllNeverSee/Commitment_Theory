## Abstract

This entry is a placeholder for one or more law review comments or online supplement essays written in response to triggering judicial decisions, regulatory developments, or scholarly debates that present an opportunity to advance CT's legal program. An appropriate trigger is a court ruling or regulatory development that raises questions bearing on: (1) AI-mediated transformation of legal text, (2) the semantic fidelity of AI systems in legal proceedings, (3) due process claims arising from AI-mediated administrative action, (4) disparate impact claims involving AI enforcement systems, or (5) evidentiary authenticity challenges to AI-transformed documents. When a trigger arises, the comment will engage the specific development, introduce CT's empirical framework as a relevant analytic tool, and demonstrate how the Conservation Law clarifies the question that the development raises. The register will match the target venue and audience: accessible to law review readers without technical background, but precise enough to be citable by future scholars and practitioners.

## Key Claims

- The triggering development raises a question that CT's empirical framework is specifically positioned to address (commitment conservation, failure mode taxonomy, measurement protocol).
- CT adds analytic precision that doctrinal tools alone cannot provide: it can distinguish measurable commitment drift from contested interpretive dispute, making the relevant legal question more tractable.
- The Conservation Law's empirical character distinguishes it from policy arguments: it makes a falsifiable claim about what the law requires that courts and agencies can evaluate on its merits.
