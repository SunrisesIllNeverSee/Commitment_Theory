## Abstract

Shannon's information theory deliberately avoided the question of meaning: a message has information content regardless of whether it means anything. This paper argues that law cannot afford the same avoidance. Legal meaning is not separable from legal effect; when AI systems transform legal text, the question of what survives transformation is not merely technical but jurisprudential. This paper introduces the Conservation Law of Commitment to the AI and Law scholarly community and argues that it provides the missing foundation for a rigorous account of semantic fidelity in AI-mediated legal processes. The paper proceeds in three parts. Part I establishes the formal framework: commitment kernel, conservation law, second law, failure taxonomy, and measurement oracle. Part II applies the framework to three core legal domains — due process in administrative AI, disparate impact in employment and housing AI, and evidentiary authenticity in AI-transformed documents — demonstrating in each domain that existing doctrine already implies a commitment conservation requirement and that CT provides its first operationally measurable formulation. Part III addresses the implications for AI and Law as a field: CT's empirical, falsifiable approach to legal meaning differs categorically from both the interpretive traditions of legal theory and the behavioral risk frameworks of AI ethics; it is the first framework for legal AI governance grounded in a physical law rather than a policy preference. The paper invites the AI and Law community to test, extend, and contest CT's empirical claims using the public harness.

## Outline

I. **Introduction: The Meaning Gap in AI and Law** — Frames the problem: AI and Law scholarship has extensively analyzed AI decision-making but has not developed a formal framework for the semantic fidelity of AI-transformed legal text.

II. **Part I — The Conservation Law: Framework and Evidence** — Presents the formal CT framework (commitment kernel, conservation law, second law, failure taxonomy, oracle) at a level appropriate for legal scholars; summarizes the empirical record (EXP-001 through EXP-007) and the failure taxonomy.

III. **Part II — Due Process: Commitment Conservation as Procedural Requirement** — Applies CT to AI-mediated administrative processes under Mathews v. Eldridge; demonstrates that commitment drift constitutes an erroneous deprivation and that the conservation law provides the technical standard for what "risk of erroneous deprivation" means in the AI context.

IV. **Part II — Disparate Impact: Commitment Drift as a Mechanism of Discrimination** — Applies CT to Griggs and its progeny; demonstrates that commitment drift in AI-mediated employment and housing enforcement is a mechanism of disparate impact — statistically measurable, structurally predictable, and governed by the conservation law.

V. **Part II — Evidentiary Authenticity: Commitment Conservation as Reliability Standard** — Applies CT to FRE 901 and Daubert; demonstrates that commitment conservation is a necessary condition for the evidentiary authenticity and scientific reliability of AI-transformed legal texts.

VI. **Part III — Implications for AI and Law** — Addresses CT's position in the AI and Law landscape: how does it relate to algorithmic accountability, explainability, fairness, and bias frameworks? What does it add that existing frameworks lack? What does it not address?

VII. **Conclusion: Invitation to the Field** — Summarizes CT's contribution to AI and Law; invites the community to test, extend, and contest the empirical claims using the public harness; identifies the most important open questions.

## Key Claims

- Legal meaning is not separable from legal effect; when AI systems transform legal text, the question of what survives transformation is a jurisprudential question, not merely a technical one.
- Existing doctrine across three independent bodies of law (due process, disparate impact, evidentiary authenticity) already implies a commitment conservation requirement; CT provides its first operationally measurable formulation.
- CT's empirical, falsifiable approach to legal meaning differs categorically from behavioral risk frameworks (AI ethics) and interpretive traditions (legal theory) — it is the first AI and Law framework grounded in a physical law.
- The AI and Law field's existing frameworks (algorithmic accountability, explainability, fairness) address AI decision-making but not the semantic fidelity of AI-transformed legal text; CT fills this gap.
- CT's empirical claims are contestable: the public harness is an invitation for the AI and Law community to replicate, stress-test, and falsify the conservation law.
