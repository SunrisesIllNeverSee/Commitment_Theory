## Abstract

This entry is a placeholder for one or more amicus briefs or regulatory comments filed in response to triggering proceedings that present appropriate occasions for CT's evidentiary record and legal framework. An appropriate trigger is a regulatory rulemaking, an agency comment period, or a judicial proceeding that raises questions bearing on: (1) the semantic fidelity of AI systems operating on legal text, (2) the evidentiary reliability of AI-transformed documents, (3) the disparate impact of AI systems in civil rights enforcement contexts, or (4) the technical standards applicable to AI governance in legal processes. When a trigger arises, the submission will introduce the Conservation Law of Commitment as a relevant empirical fact, cite L-001 and L-003 for the doctrinal and technical standard frameworks, cite Paper 0 for the empirical record, and argue that the proceeding's outcome should be consistent with the commitment conservation requirement already implied by existing doctrine. The submission will be written for a legal or regulatory audience unfamiliar with CT, will avoid technical jargon, and will emphasize the falsifiability and empirical basis of CT's claims as distinguishing features relative to other governance frameworks in the same proceeding.

## Writing Notes

**Peer review status gate — CRITICAL:**
Key Claim 3 states CT's empirical record is "peer-reviewed (or under review)." Daubert's peer-review factor does not treat "under review" as satisfying the peer-review requirement. This parenthetical is vulnerable and must be removed or replaced before any filing. **Status gate: do not file L-006 until Paper 0 has achieved formal peer review or conference acceptance at a recognized venue.** If filed before peer review, rewrite Key Claim 3 to say: "CT's empirical record is publicly available, replicable, and falsifiable" — omitting the peer-review claim entirely and relying on Daubert's other three factors (falsifiability, known error rate, general acceptance).
