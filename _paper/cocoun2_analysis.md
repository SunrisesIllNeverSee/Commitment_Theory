## Finding 5 — THE BULLET TO DODGE: "AI output lacks human mental impressions"

CoCounsel identified the strongest counterargument we should preemptively address. The argument has four legs:

1. **Hickman/Nobles foundation:** Work product exists to protect human cognition, not algorithmic output.
2. **Roberts v. Americable, 883 F. Supp. 499 (E.D. Cal. 1995):** Pre-AI analog — denied work-product protection to tape recordings because they "only reveal the thought processes of the conversants" and "the attorneys were not at any time a conversant."
3. **Thaler v. Perlmutter, 130 F.4th 1039 (D.C. Cir. 2025):** Copyright's human-authorship requirement extended to AI. Cross-doctrinal analogy.
4. **Riverkeeper, 2024 WL 1053507 (N.D.N.Y.):** Denied protection where non-attorney lacked "qualifications to be a consultant or agent" under Rule 26(b)(3).

**Why this is the bullet:** A reviewer who reads our paper without seeing this counterargument addressed will wonder "but what about Roberts/Thaler — doesn't AI output lack the human mental impressions the doctrine actually protects?"

**Our response (which we already have, just need to surface):** Our functional rule does not claim AI generates protectable mental impressions; it claims **the human's preparation activity** (the prompts, the directional choices, the use of the output to develop strategy) is what merits protection. The AI is the substrate; the protected work is the human's preparation in anticipation of litigation. This is exactly the Warner court's "tools, not persons" reasoning, plus our extension to crime-victim and pro se contexts.

**Recommended addition:** A short counterargument paragraph in Part V (currently 5 paragraphs; add a 6th between paragraphs 2 and 3), roughly 60-80 words:

> *Critics may object that this functional rule extends work-product protection to materials that lack the human mental impressions the doctrine was designed to shelter. See Roberts v. Americable Int'l, Inc., 883 F. Supp. 499 (E.D. Cal. 1995); cf. Thaler v. Perlmutter, 130 F.4th 1039 (D.C. Cir. 2025). The objection is misplaced. The functional rule does not protect AI output as such; it protects the preparation activity—the prompts, the directional choices, the use of output in service of litigation strategy—which embeds the human preparer's mental impressions just as surely as a paralegal's research memorandum does.*

This addition would:
- Adds ~75 words → new word count ~5,008 (over by ~8 — would need a small trim elsewhere OR keep just under 5,000 by tightening the surrounding paragraphs)
- Adds two new authorities (Roberts, Thaler) we haven't cited yet
- **Significantly strengthens the paper against editor "missed counterargument" feedback**

**Recommendation:** Do this if word budget allows. If not, at minimum add Roberts and Thaler as a "see also" in fn 30 with the response telegraphed in Part V's existing language.
