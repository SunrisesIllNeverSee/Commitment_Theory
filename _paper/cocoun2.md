When a non-attorney prompts an AI system to analyze documents, draft summaries, or identify relevant facts for litigation, the resulting output reflects the AI's algorithmic processing — not the thought process of a human legal actor.

The text of Rule 26(b)(3) extends protection to materials prepared by a party's "representative (including the other party's attorney, consultant, surety, indemnitor, insurer, or agent)" FRCP Rule 26. Courts have consistently held that non-attorney humans can qualify under this list — but only when they occupy a recognized legal role in relation to the party or its counsel. And if the operator is a non-attorney who has not been directed by counsel and whose work does not reflect counsel's strategy, then even the "tool" framing leads to the conclusion that no protected mental impressions of a legal representative are embedded in the output.
Structural Analogy: The Human Authorship Requirement in Copyright Law

The cross-doctrinal analogy from copyright law reinforces the counterargument. In Thaler v. Perlmutter, 130 F.4th 1039 (D.C. Cir. 2025), the D.C. This copyright framework maps directly onto the work-product counterargument. Just as copyright protection requires a human author whose creative expression is embedded in the work, work-product protection requires a human legal actor whose mental impressions are embedded in the materials. When a non-attorney operates AI to produce litigation-related documents without attorney direction, the human contribution is limited to the prompt — an act that courts would likely characterize as insufficient to supply the embedded professional judgment that work-product doctrine protects. The AI generates the substantive analysis, just as it generates expressive text in the copyright context. Neither product is the expression of a qualifying human mind acting in a protected legal capacity.

Practical Implications

The counterargument against extending protection has immediate consequences for discovery practice. Litigants who routinely direct non-attorneys to use AI tools for document review, factual synthesis, or pre-litigation analysis — without attorney supervision — risk having all such materials treated as ordinary-course business records subject to full disclosure. The safest practice is for attorneys to retain direct supervision over AI-assisted work product: AI tools should be deployed at counsel's express direction, framed as a component of the attorney's litigation preparation process, and the output should be reviewed and refined to ensure it reflects the attorney's analytical judgment rather than standing as raw algorithmic output. Non-attorneys — paralegals, forensic consultants, in-house investigators — who use AI independently of counsel cannot point to their AI-generated materials as embodying attorney mental impressions, because neither the non-attorney nor the AI has exercised the kind of human legal cognition the doctrine protects. Parties must also be cautious about submitting AI-generated materials to third-party AI platforms, as the waiver analysis — while generally favorable under Warner and Morgan — remains unsettled and circuit-specific. Firms drafting privilege logs should document the attorney's directional role and the litigation purpose of any AI-assisted research or analysis, since bare assertions that materials were "prepared in anticipation of litigation" will not satisfy courts that look for embedded attorney mental impressions before applying protection.

Commentary on This Question

The work-product doctrine under the Federal Rules of Civil Procedure 26(b)(3) provides a qualified immunity from discovery for materials prepared in anticipation of litigation by or for a party or its representative. Although traditionally associated with attorneys, the doctrine extends protection to materials prepared by non-attorneys so long as they act as agents or representatives aiding in litigation preparation. This rationale rests on preserving a “zone of privacy” enabling strategic legal planning free from adversarial intrusion and is grounded in practical litigation realities where attorneys rely on non-attorney agents to develop case materials. The doctrine protects both tangible and intangible work product, including mental impressions and conclusions of attorneys, with stronger protection for opinion work product, which may only be disclosed under rare and exceptional circumstances or substantial need with undue hardship shown 39 AMJUR POF 3d 1, FEDTRHB-CIV § 5:8, PRIVILEGES § 2:2.
Nonetheless, courts have drawn critical distinctions where non-attorney materials do not qualify, especially if materials are prepared for business or regulatory compliance purposes unrelated to litigation or without attorney involvement reflecting legal strategies. Non-attorney preparation may lose protection if the preparation lacks a clear nexus to litigation anticipation or is used primarily for non-litigation business functions. Further, courts emphasize the necessity of a showing that materials would not have been created “but for” the prospect of litigation. Protection also depends on whether the non-attorney agent acted at counsel’s behest in assisting litigation or was merely performing routine business tasks. The comparative lack of direct attorney involvement or the materials’ commercial purpose form the strongest counterarguments against extending work-product protection to AI-assisted, non-attorney-prepared materials 4 AMJUR TRIALS 223, ANPATDIG § 42:161, PRIVILEGES § 2:13.

The Current Landscape of AI Work-Product Scholarship

Since 2024, legal scholarship on AI and work product has focused almost exclusively on two doctrinal questions: (1) whether AI-generated or AI-assisted materials qualify for protection under the attorney work-product doctrine codified in Federal Rule of Civil Procedure 26(b)(3); and (2) what professional responsibility obligations accompany an attorney's use of AI tools in drafting litigation materials. Courts have grappled with both issues in reported opinions, but scholarship on either has not crossed into constitutional civil rights territory.
On the doctrinal side, courts have begun to resolve threshold questions about AI-generated materials and the work-product doctrine. In Warner v. This holding was echoed in subsequent federal district court decisions. Conversely, in United States v. These decisions have established a nascent federal jurisprudence on AI and work product, but the opinions are grounded exclusively in the traditional elements of the work-product doctrine — anticipation of litigation, the mental processes of counsel, and the risk of adversarial disclosure — with no constitutional overlay. Neither the courts in these cases nor commentary surrounding them has engaged with the question of whether differential access to AI legal tools, or differential judicial treatment of AI-generated work product, raises equal protection or due process concerns.

The Adjacent Scholarship That Does Exist

The body of scholarship and regulatory guidance that comes closest to framing AI as a civil rights issue concerns different applications of AI — specifically, algorithmic decision-making in employment, housing, consumer lending, and criminal sentencing — not the work-product doctrine. The EEOC, DOJ, FTC, and CFPB issued joint statements in 2023 and 2024 on the civil rights dimensions of algorithmic bias, and the Biden administration's OMB Memorandum M-25-21 directed federal agencies to conduct AI impact assessments addressing civil rights and nondiscrimination concerns. These materials treat the civil rights implications of AI as arising from its role in consequential decision-making processes that affect protected classes — not from the application of evidence law principles like the work-product doctrine to AI-generated litigation materials.
Practical Law research materials confirm this pattern. The intersection of AI and civil rights in the legal literature runs through Title VII disparate-impact claims, ADA algorithmic-bias challenges, and Fair Housing Act concerns — not through the lens of who may or may not claim work-product protection for AI-assisted documents Practice notes, AI in the Workplace (US). The significant volume of recent EEOC guidance, including the now-rescinded 2022 and 2023 technical assistance documents on AI in employment decisions, treated algorithmic fairness as a statutory civil rights matter rather than a constitutional one.
Why the Gap Exists

The absence of law review scholarship connecting AI work product to equal protection, due process, or access to courts likely reflects structural features of both the doctrine and the scholarship ecosystem. The work-product doctrine, rooted in Hickman v. Taylor and codified in Rule 26(b)(3), is primarily a procedural and evidentiary concept that governs discovery in litigation; its civil rights dimensions are non-obvious. The constitutional right of access to courts has been litigated primarily in prisoner and pro se contexts, and the connection between that right and an attorney's ability to use AI tools in work-product creation is not a natural doctrinal step without considerable theoretical construction. Moreover, generative AI tools in litigation are new enough — the wave of sanctioned AI hallucination cases beginning in 2023 is barely two years old — that law reviews have not yet fully processed the range of doctrinal frames through which the phenomenon might be analyzed.
There is also a mismatch in scholarly attention. The civil rights AI scholarship — which is extensive — focuses on AI used against people (discriminatory screening algorithms, biased risk-assessment tools in criminal sentencing, exclusionary hiring systems). The AI work-product scholarship — which is growing rapidly — focuses on AI used by people in litigation. No existing law review article has bridged these two bodies of thought by asking whether the benefits of AI as a litigation tool are themselves distributed in ways that implicate constitutional guarantees of equal treatment or meaningful court access.
Practical Implications

The absence of such scholarship does not mean the theoretical arguments are unavailable. The constitutional right of access to courts has been recognized as grounded in multiple constitutional provisions, including the Confrontation Clause of the Sixth Amendment, the Due Process Clause, the Sixth Amendment right to trial by a jury composed of a fair cross section of the community, and the First Amendment right of access to criminal proceedings, which collectively ensure that individuals have a meaningful opportunity to seek judicial redress
