Federal courts have begun dividing on whether AI-assisted legal preparation is protected work product — and the divide is not about AI. It is about who is doing the preparing. In United States v. Heppner, a court held that a defendant’s AI-generated legal analysis was discoverable because it was not created under counsel’s direction. Within days of each other, in Warner v. Gilbarco, Inc., a court held that a pro se litigant’s AI-assisted materials were protected because they reflected her own litigation strategy. Together, these decisions expose a growing fracture: materially similar legal preparation receives different protection based not on the nature of the work, but on the status of the person performing it. This is not a new narrative. Law has long conditioned access to protection on credential and resource. What is new is the infrastructure through which that condition now operates.

AI introduces a new kind of variation that the system has not yet accounted for. It is not doctrinal failure. It is a new condition requiring new infrastructure. This Essay argues that the resulting divide raises an underexamined civil rights problem. Legal systems have long protected certain forms of preparation because effective participation in litigation requires space to think, test arguments, organize facts, and develop strategy without compelled disclosure. If protection depends on whether the user is represented, licensed, or using an enterprise platform, then materially similar legal preparation may receive different treatment based on professional status and technological access. That result risks burdening the very people most likely to rely on AI: criminal defendants, pro se litigants, administrative claimants, and others navigating legal systems without institutional support.

This Essay contends that the emerging treatment of AI-assisted legal preparation implicates core concerns of civil rights law: equal protection, procedural fairness, and meaningful access to legal institutions. By “civil rights,” this Essay refers both to constitutional guarantees of equal protection, due process, and access to courts, and to the broader civil rights tradition of identifying structural arrangements that preserve inequality despite formal legal equality. The Essay proposes a functional rule: work product protection should attach to legal preparation made in anticipation of proceedings, not to the professional identity of the preparer or the technological channel through which the work is produced.

Revised Introduction Opening

In February 2026, a divide became visible in federal work-product doctrine. The question was not whether lawyers may use AI. The question was whether a person’s AI-assisted legal preparation—research, factual analysis, strategic planning, and draft argument—remains protected when created through a publicly available AI platform. In Heppner, the answer was no. In Warner, the answer was yes. Together, the cases reveal a problem that doctrine has not yet absorbed: AI has introduced a new condition into legal preparation, and courts have begun to classify that condition through inherited categories that were not built for it.

This is not a new narrative about technology replacing lawyers. Nor is it another warning that artificial intelligence will disrupt legal practice. The deeper problem is infrastructural. Work-product doctrine protects legal preparation because litigation requires a protected zone for thought. But when that zone is mediated by AI, courts must decide whether protection follows the function of the work or the status of the person and platform through which the work passes.

Cleaner Heppner Paragraph

In United States v. Heppner, the Southern District of New York held that a criminal defendant’s AI-generated legal documents were not protected work product. Bradley Heppner, facing securities-fraud charges, used a consumer AI platform to create documents containing defense strategies, factual analyses, and legal arguments. He did so without his attorney’s direction. The court emphasized the traditional core of the work-product doctrine: protecting an attorney’s mental processes and preserving a zone in which counsel can analyze and prepare a case. Because the materials were generated by a non-lawyer without attorney involvement, the court held that they fell outside that protection. The platform’s terms of service, which allowed data use and disclosure, reinforced the court’s conclusion that Heppner lacked a reasonable expectation of confidentiality. The documents were therefore discoverable.  

Cleaner Warner Paragraph

Warner v. Gilbarco, Inc. reached the opposite result. Sohyon Warner, a pro se plaintiff in an employment-discrimination case, used ChatGPT to assist with litigation preparation. The defendants sought production of materials related to that use, arguing that any protection had been waived by disclosure to a third-party AI platform. The court denied the motion. It reasoned that a pro se litigant occupies a dual role: both party and advocate. Her AI-assisted materials therefore reflected her own legal preparation in anticipation of litigation. On that basis, the court treated the AI tool as a tool, not as an independent legal actor whose involvement destroyed protection.
