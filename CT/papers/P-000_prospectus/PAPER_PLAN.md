## Abstract

Commitment Theory (CT) is a formal framework for the governance of semantic content under transformation. This prospectus deposits twelve foundational propositions that stake the CT claim publicly, define all terms for downstream citation, map a 31-paper research program, and invite falsification. The central object of study is the commitment kernel C(S) — the deontic core of a semantic object, comprising its obligations, prohibitions, permissions, and modal constraints. The central law is the Conservation Law of Commitment: C(T_gov(S)) = C(S), which states that under governed transformation the commitment kernel is conserved, while under ungoverned transformation it decays monotonically (the Second Law of Semantic Entropy, ΔH_C > 0). The twelve propositions cover: the formal definition of the commitment kernel; the Conservation Law statement and experimental record; a whitespace claim (nine novel terms with zero academic collision as of the date of deposit, verified against seven academic databases); the Second Law as a candidate physical law; a nine-mode failure taxonomy; oracle independence; the public harness as an invitation to falsify; and cross-domain applications spanning law, information science, AI governance, and communication theory. Appendices provide search-method evidence for the whitespace claim and a disambiguation table distinguishing CT's deontic, signal-level commitment from linguistics' epistemic, speaker-level commitment. P-000 is not a traditional paper; it is a timestamped, citable origin point for a research program that others may build on, compete with, or refute.

## Key Claims

- The commitment kernel C(S) is formally definable, operationally measurable, and distinct from meaning-in-general; it is specifically the action-binding deontic content of a semantic object.
- Under governed transformation (Six-Gate Protocol applied), C(S) is conserved across recursive iterations — this is a discovered law, not a design choice, and it is falsifiable.
- Under ungoverned transformation, the commitment kernel decays monotonically (ΔH_C > 0); this decay is the semantic analog of thermodynamic entropy increase.
- CT occupies a genuine whitespace in the academic literature: nine core terms have zero collision with prior usage at the time of deposit.
- CT's "commitment" is categorically distinct from the epistemic, speaker-level "commitment" used throughout linguistics and dialogue theory; one sentence establishes the categorical separation.
