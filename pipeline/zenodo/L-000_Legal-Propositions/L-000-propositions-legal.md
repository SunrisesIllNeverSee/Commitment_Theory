## Preface

The Conservation Law of Commitment — first introduced as an empirical finding in information theory — holds that the commitment kernel of a signal (its obligations, prohibitions, and modal constraints) is conserved under governed transformation and decays under ungoverned transformation. This law has been tested across seven experimental series comprising 57 signals and 181 condition-signal runs, producing a nine-mode failure taxonomy and a three-regime classification of signal behavior under recursive compression.[^1]

This document does not restate the law or its empirical record. It maps a research program that investigates what the law means for legal systems — systems that depend, at every level, on the assumption that meaning remains stable across documents, translations, interpretations, and time.

The propositions below are grounded in the Conservation Law but make claims that the law itself does not make. They are legal claims, not information-theoretic ones. Each proposition corresponds to one or more planned papers in the research program mapped at the end of this document.

---

## The Propositions

**Proposition 1. Legal meaning carries a commitment kernel that is operationally measurable.**

Every legal text — statute, regulation, contract, judicial opinion, evidentiary record — carries commitments: obligations imposed, prohibitions enforced, permissions granted, conditions specified, exceptions carved. These commitments are the binding force of the text. They are not the only thing the text means, but they are the part that must survive for the text to remain legally operative.

The commitment kernel is the minimal invariant that preserves this binding force. It can be extracted, measured, and compared across transformations using an independent oracle. The kernel is not a summary. It is not a paraphrase. It is the irreducible core of legal obligation.[^2]

---

**Proposition 2. Under ungoverned AI transformation, the commitment kernel of legal text degrades in characteristic, predictable ways.**

When legal text is processed by AI systems without governance constraints — summarized, paraphrased, compressed, translated, or abstracted — the commitment kernel is subject to measurable decay. This decay is not random. It follows characteristic failure modes documented across the experimental record:

Obligation escalation: a discretionary provision ("should") becomes mandatory ("must"). A permission becomes a requirement. A safe harbor becomes an obligation.

Scope widening: a narrow prohibition ("you must not enter room A") expands into a broad ban ("you must not enter any room"). A specific exemption generalizes into a categorical rule.

Exception dropping: a statutory defense ("unless doing so would impose an undue hardship") is omitted entirely. The qualifying clause that limits a prohibition disappears. The exception that protects a party vanishes.

Modal frame inversion: a prohibition ("shall not sublease without written consent") becomes a requirement ("obtain tenant consent"). The negative frame flips to positive. The legal meaning reverses.

Each failure mode has direct doctrinal consequences. An escalated obligation creates liability where none existed. A widened scope extends a restriction beyond its legislative intent. A dropped exception eliminates a defense that Congress or a legislature deliberately provided. A frame inversion transforms the nature of the legal relationship.[^3]

---

**Proposition 3. The Conservation Law of Commitment holds on legal text.**

The same law that governs commitment preservation in general signals governs legal signals. Under governed transformation — transformation that passes through a verification protocol designed to preserve the commitment kernel — legal text retains its binding force across recursive iterations. Under ungoverned transformation, it degrades.

This proposition is empirically testable. A corpus of legal signals — provisions of the Civil Rights Act, ADA requirements, Fair Housing Act clauses, regulatory text, contract provisions — can be subjected to the same recursive transformation protocol used in the existing experimental series. The prediction: governed transformation conserves the commitment kernel of legal text; ungoverned transformation does not.

This is the central empirical claim of the research program and the subject of a dedicated study.[^4]

---

**Proposition 4. Existing legal doctrine already implies a commitment conservation requirement — it simply has not been named.**

Due process requires meaningful notice and an opportunity to be heard. Under Mathews v. Eldridge, the risk of erroneous deprivation is a factor in evaluating the adequacy of procedures.[^5] An AI system that generates a benefits denial letter without preserving the commitment kernel of the governing regulation introduces a quantifiable, systemic risk of erroneous deprivation. The due process requirement of meaningful notice is, at bottom, a requirement that the meaning of the law survive the system that communicates it.

Evidentiary authentication under Federal Rule of Evidence 901 requires that the proponent demonstrate an item of evidence "is what the proponent claims it is."[^6] An AI-generated summary of a legal document that does not preserve the commitment kernel of the original is not what it claims to be. The authentication requirement is, at bottom, a commitment conservation requirement.

Disparate impact analysis under Title VII requires that an employment practice be "job-related for the position in question and consistent with business necessity."[^7] An AI system that operationalizes this standard but drops the "business necessity" qualifier through ungoverned transformation is not applying the legal standard at all. It is applying a semantically distinct standard. The disparate impact framework presupposes that the standard being tested has survived its operationalization.

Each of these doctrinal requirements already demands what the Conservation Law describes. What is missing is not a new legal rule but a formal recognition that the preservation of legal meaning across AI transformation is a measurable, enforceable condition — and that there exists a law that governs it.[^8]

---

**Proposition 5. A commitment conservation requirement would formalize what existing doctrine already demands.**

The research program proposes that any AI system applying a legal provision should be required to demonstrate that the commitment kernel of that provision has been preserved across its transformation pipeline. This is a commitment conservation requirement.

The requirement is not a transparency mandate. The model need not be opened. It is a fidelity mandate. The output must demonstrably preserve the input's commitment kernel.

The requirement is analogous to chain of custody for physical evidence. Chain of custody accounts for evidence from collection to courtroom, ensuring it has not been tampered with or substituted. A commitment conservation requirement accounts for legal meaning from source to application, ensuring it has not been eroded or transformed.

The requirement is enforceable through a governance protocol — a set of verification gates that any AI system can implement — and auditable through a cryptographic fidelity seal that certifies the result without revealing proprietary internals.[^9]

This is not a proposal without precedent. It is a proposal that names and operationalizes what courts, agencies, and legislatures have always required: that the law means what it says, and that what it says survives the systems that carry it.

---

**Proposition 6. Pluralism is conserved, not erased.**

The framework does not impose a single correct meaning on any legal text. It does not resolve interpretive disputes. It does not adjudicate between competing readings of a statute. If two courts read a provision differently, the framework preserves both readings as distinct commitment kernels, each with its own lineage and fidelity record.

What the framework eliminates is not disagreement but undetected degradation — the silent erosion of meaning that occurs when AI systems transform legal text without governance. Interpretive pluralism is a feature of legal systems. Semantic drift is a threat to them. The Conservation Law distinguishes between the two: a preserved kernel that is read differently by different courts is pluralism; a degraded kernel that no longer carries its original obligations is entropy.

The framework operates at the level of record integrity, not substantive adjudication. It answers: has the meaning of this text been preserved through its transformations? It does not answer: what is the correct meaning of this text? This distinction is constitutive. The framework is a law of the record, not a law of interpretation.[^10]

---

## Destination

Taken together, these propositions point toward a conclusion this research program is designed to reach: that the Conservation Law of Commitment constitutes the foundation for a new jurisprudence of the record — a constitutional framework for ensuring that legal meaning survives the systems that process it.

No existing AI governance framework makes this claim because no existing framework has a law to ground it in. The Conservation Law of Commitment is not a policy recommendation. It is not an ethical guideline. It is not a set of best practices. It is a falsifiable, empirically tested physical law with a public harness, a documented experimental record, and a patent-pending enforcement architecture. It is the only governance foundation that operates as physics rather than policy.

The claim that this law supports a new jurisprudence is not argued here. It is the destination. The research program mapped below is the path.

## Research Program

The following papers develop, test, and apply the propositions above. Each paper is identified by its track number, and full details are maintained in the CCT Publication Roadmap.

**L-001 | Stanford Law Review Online Essay.** Introduces the commitment conservation framework to legal academia through a 4,500-word essay applying it to civil rights enforcement — disparate impact, due process, and evidentiary reliability. Develops Propositions 4 and 5 in compressed form. (Submitted to SLRO Special Collection: Technology, AI, and the Future of Civil Rights. Deadline: May 1, 2026.)

**L-002 | Empirical Legal Study.** Tests Proposition 3 directly by running a corpus of civil rights statutes, regulations, and contract provisions through the recursive transformation harness. Reports drift rates, failure mode distribution, and vulnerability rankings for legal text under governed and ungoverned conditions.

**L-003 | Technical Standard Paper.** Develops Proposition 5 in full — the commitment conservation requirement as a proposed legal standard, with the chain-of-custody analogy, the governance protocol, and the fidelity seal mechanism. Informed by L-002's legal-specific data.

**L-004 | Policy Brief.** Translates the commitment conservation requirement into policy language for the EU AI Act, NIST AI RMF, and FTC enforcement guidance. Positions the Conservation Law as the missing physics beneath existing governance policy.

**L-005 | Observational Legal Paper.** Presents Proposition 2's failure modes in legal terms — each mode as a doctrinal scenario with specific consequences for liability, compliance, and enforcement. Can merge with L-002.

**L-006 | Amicus Brief / Regulatory Comment.** Applies the framework to a live regulatory proceeding or judicial case. Opportunistic — held in reserve for the right triggering event.

**L-007 | Interdisciplinary Full Paper.** The comprehensive treatment — Conservation Law, Second Law, governance protocol, all legal applications. Targets Artificial Intelligence and Law or equivalent venue. Written after L-001 is published and citable.

**L-008 | Legal Theory Capstone.** The destination paper. Argues that the Conservation Law of Commitment constitutes the foundation for a jurisprudence of the record. Grounded in the full evidence base of L-001 through L-007. Written with legal co-author. The boldest claim, earned by everything that precedes it.

**L-009 | Law Review Comment.** Responsive commentary on a specific judicial ruling involving AI-generated evidence, algorithmic decision-making, or AI-assisted legal drafting. Applies commitment conservation to the ruling. Opportunistic.

**L-010 | Doctrinal Analysis.** Pure legal reasoning — Daubert, FRE 901, Mathews v. Eldridge — applied to AI-transformed legal texts. Primary authorship by legal collaborator. McHenry provides the science.
