## Abstract

[Documentation of submitted paper — not a draft.]

Civil rights enforcement increasingly passes through AI systems that transform legal text — statutes, standards, regulations, administrative decisions — before it reaches the people those texts were written to protect. This paper argues that the legal system must confront a structural problem it does not yet have a name for: the degradation of the semantic content of civil rights law under AI-mediated transformation. Two scenarios anchor the analysis. In the first, a Title VII employment algorithm compresses "unless the employer demonstrates a business necessity" out of the disparate impact standard during AI-mediated compliance processing — not through discriminatory intent but through commitment drift across transformation steps. In the second, a benefits-denial notice transforms "shall not" into "may not" and then "may" across three AI processing steps, converting a statutory prohibition into a discretionary permission before the claimant reads it. These scenarios are not hypothetical edge cases; they are the predictable consequence of deploying AI transformation systems without a governance framework that addresses semantic continuity. Section V introduces the Conservation Law of Commitment — not as a tool or proposal, but as a discovered fact about how meaning behaves under transformation — and shows that civil rights doctrine already implies a commitment conservation requirement through Mathews v. Eldridge's risk-of-erroneous-deprivation analysis, Griggs's disparate impact standard, and FRE 901's authentication requirements. The paper concludes that the uncertainty created by AI's semantic opacity is not a reason to wait; it is an invitation to build the governance infrastructure civil rights enforcement requires.

## Outline

I. **The Problem Has a Shape** — Introduces commitment drift through the two anchor scenarios; establishes that the problem is structural rather than incidental, a consequence of how AI transformation works rather than how any particular system was designed.

II. **What Civil Rights Law Already Requires** — Analyzes Mathews v. Eldridge (due process, risk of erroneous deprivation), Griggs v. Duke Power Co. (disparate impact, business necessity defense), and FRE 901 (authentication, evidentiary reliability) as existing doctrinal sources of a commitment conservation requirement.

III. **Why Existing AI Governance Frameworks Are Insufficient** — Demonstrates that Constitutional AI, NIST AI RMF, and EU AI Act do not address commitment drift; they govern behavior and risk at the system level, not semantic continuity at the transformation level.

IV. **The Doctrinal Gap and Its Consequences** — Characterizes the gap between what existing doctrine requires and what existing governance frameworks provide; identifies the populations most exposed to commitment drift (unrepresented parties, pro se litigants, benefits claimants, non-English speakers).

V. **The Conservation Law of Commitment** — Introduces CT in three paragraphs: (1) what the Conservation Law states and what it implies, (2) the empirical record (EXP-003, EXP-007 briefly described), (3) the operational components (Six-Gate Protocol, public harness). Framed as a discovered fact, not a proposal.

VI. **The Uncertainty That Invites Building** — Argues that the doctrinal uncertainty created by AI's semantic opacity is an invitation to build governance infrastructure rather than a reason to delay; sketches the minimum legal requirements a commitment conservation standard must satisfy.

## Key Claims

- AI systems routinely transform civil rights legal text in ways that degrade the commitment kernel — the action-binding deontic content — through a process of commitment drift that is structurally predictable, not incidentally aberrant.
- Existing civil rights doctrine (Mathews v. Eldridge, Griggs, FRE 901) already implies a commitment conservation requirement; CT makes that implication explicit and provides the measurement framework to operationalize it.
- Existing AI governance frameworks (Constitutional AI, NIST AI RMF, EU AI Act) do not address commitment drift because they operate at the system level rather than the transformation level.
- The populations most exposed to commitment drift — unrepresented parties, pro se litigants, benefits claimants — are the same populations civil rights law was designed to protect; the doctrinal failure and the governance failure align.
- The Conservation Law of Commitment is a discovered fact about how meaning behaves under transformation; civil rights enforcement must account for it whether or not the legal system adopts CT's vocabulary.
