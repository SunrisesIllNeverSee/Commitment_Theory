## Q3. What was NOT AI-generated?

The author originated and is solely responsible for:

- The identification of the *Heppner / Warner / Morgan* doctrinal fracture as a civil rights problem rather than a privilege problem.
- The civil rights framing connecting work-product doctrine to the *Griffin* / *Gideon* / *Faretta* lineage and to equal protection, procedural fairness, and access-to-courts principles.
- The proposed functional rule (Part V) tying protection to the nature of preparation rather than the credential of the preparer.
- The crime-victim gap analysis (Part IV.C) recognizing that crime victims fall outside both the represented-party and self-represented-litigant frameworks.
- The preemptive response to the "AI lacks human mental impressions" counterargument (Part V), distinguishing the work-product preparation activity from algorithmic output and from the *Thaler v. Perlmutter* copyright authorship requirement.
- The Conservation Law of Commitment (Part VI) and its experimental record. Both Zenodo deposits predate any AI-assisted drafting of this Essay.

Independent CoCounsel/Westlaw research confirmed that no law review article published since 2024 has framed the AI work-product issue as implicating equal protection, procedural due process, or access to courts. The civil rights AI scholarship to date concerns AI used *against* people; the AI work-product scholarship concerns AI used *by* people. This Essay bridges those two bodies of thought.

The following AI-generated citation errors were identified and corrected before submission:

- ***Warner*** — initial draft cited `No. 23-12531, 2026 WL 460146`. Corrected to `No. 2:24-cv-12333, 2026 WL 373043`.
- ***Morgan v. V2X, Inc.*** — initial draft cited `No. 24-CV-03199, 2026 WL 1069587`. Corrected to `No. 25-cv-01991-SKC-MDB, 2026 WL 864223`.
- ***Shih v. Petal Card, Inc.*** — initial draft cited `2021 WL 1966870 (JPC)`. Corrected to the published reporter cite `565 F. Supp. 3d 557, 574 (S.D.N.Y. 2021)`. Judge initials corrected (JPC → JFK).
- ***Carpenter v. United States*** — initial parenthetical described the holding as "distinguishing content from routing information." Corrected to mirror the actual holding (Headnote 14: diminished privacy interests in third-party-shared information do not categorically defeat constitutional protection). Pin cite added at 138 S.
